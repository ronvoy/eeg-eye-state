PART 1 — THE BIG PICTURE
===============================================================================

--- 1.1 WHAT IS EEG? ---

EEG stands for Electroencephalography. Break down the word:
- "Electro" means electricity
- "encephalo" means brain
- "graphy" means recording

So EEG is a recording of the brain's electricity.

How does it work? Your brain has billions of neurons. When neurons fire,
they produce tiny electrical signals. Put enough neurons together and
those signals are strong enough to detect from OUTSIDE the skull — by
placing metal sensors (electrodes) on the scalp.

Imagine putting a microphone near a crowd. You can't hear one person
clearly, but you can hear the overall roar of the crowd. EEG is similar:
you can't see one neuron firing, but you can see the rhythm of millions
firing together.


--- 1.2 WHAT PROBLEM ARE WE SOLVING? ---

Simple statement: given a short slice of brain signals, decide whether
the person had their EYES OPEN or EYES CLOSED.

Why is this hard? Because eye state is NOT directly visible in the
signal. There's no obvious "switch" that flips. You have to look at
subtle patterns — like which frequencies have more energy, or how
different parts of the brain coordinate.

Why is this useful? Detecting eye state is the foundation for:
- Drowsiness detection in drivers
- Sleep stage classification for sleep labs
- Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs)
- Fatigue monitoring in aviation and industry


--- 1.3 WHAT'S A DATASET? ---

A dataset is a big table. Each row is one measurement; each column is
one thing we measured.

For our project, each row is ONE SAMPLE (one moment in time, captured
at 1/128 of a second). The columns are:
- The 14 EEG channels (one column each)
- The label: 0 (eyes open) or 1 (eyes closed)

Our dataset has 14,980 rows. So we have 14,980 moments in time, each
with 14 channel values and one label.


--- 1.4 WHAT'S A MODEL? ---

A model is a function. You give it an input, it gives you an output.

For us:
- Input: a slice of brain signals (64 samples × 14 channels, for DL)
  or one row of features (for classical ML)
- Output: a probability between 0 and 1
  - Close to 0 = eyes probably open
  - Close to 1 = eyes probably closed

The model has millions (or only 400, for EEGNet) of tunable numbers
called PARAMETERS. During training, these numbers are adjusted so that
the model's outputs match the true labels as well as possible.


===============================================================================
PART 2 — THE HARDWARE
===============================================================================

--- 2.1 WHAT'S AN ELECTRODE? ---

An electrode is a small metal disc that picks up electrical signals.
For EEG, electrodes sit on the scalp, held in place by a cap or
headband, with a bit of salt water or gel to help conductivity.

Research labs use silver/silver-chloride electrodes with conductive gel.
Our dataset used the Emotiv EPOC — a consumer-grade headset with felt
pads soaked in salt water. Cheaper, less reliable, lower signal quality.


--- 2.2 WHAT'S A MICROVOLT? ---

A volt is a unit of electrical potential.
A microvolt (µV) is one millionth of a volt.

A AA battery is about 1.5 volts. A brain wave is about 10 to 100
microvolts — that's 10,000 to 100,000 times weaker than a battery.
This is why EEG is noisy: you're measuring tiny signals that can be
easily swamped by interference.


--- 2.3 WHAT'S A SAMPLING RATE? ---

Sampling means taking a measurement at a specific moment in time.
Sampling rate is how MANY measurements per second.

Our dataset: 128 Hz means 128 samples every second. So each sample
represents 1/128 = 7.8 milliseconds of brain activity.

Why 128? Rule of thumb: you need a sampling rate at least twice the
highest frequency you want to measure (this is the Nyquist rule —
more on that in 6.4). For EEG we typically care about frequencies up
to 50 or 60 Hz. 128 Hz is double that with margin.


--- 2.4 WHAT'S THE 10-20 SYSTEM? ---

Imagine you want to place electrodes on heads of different sizes
consistently. You need a system.

The 10-20 system (created in 1958) does this:
1. Measure the distance from the bridge of the nose to the base of
   the skull at the back. Call this distance D.
2. Place electrodes at 10% and 20% of D, at specific positions.

This way, "F7" on a small head is anatomically at the same place as
"F7" on a large head — always over the same brain region.

Naming convention:
- First letter = lobe (F=frontal, C=central, T=temporal, P=parietal,
  O=occipital)
- Number = which hemisphere (odd=left, even=right)
- Example: "F7" = left frontal area, lateral position


--- 2.5 WHAT ARE THE 5 EEG BANDS? ---

Brain waves oscillate at different speeds. Neuroscientists group them
into 5 bands, each associated with a mental state:

DELTA (0.5 to 4 Hz): deep sleep, unconsciousness. Very slow waves.
THETA (4 to 8 Hz): drowsiness, meditation, memory work.
ALPHA (8 to 12 Hz): relaxed wakefulness with eyes closed. ★
BETA (12 to 30 Hz): active thinking, focused attention, problem-solving.
GAMMA (30 Hz and up): high-level cognition, feature binding.

Think of it like music: delta is a slow bass drone, gamma is a rapid
violin. Your brain is playing all of them simultaneously, but the
"loudness" of each changes with your mental state.


--- 2.6 WHAT'S THE BERGER EFFECT? ---

In 1924, Hans Berger invented EEG. By 1929 he discovered something
striking: when a person closes their eyes, a strong rhythm around 10 Hz
appears in the back of the head (the visual cortex).

This rhythm is the ALPHA wave. When your eyes are open and you're
looking at things, the visual cortex is busy processing what you see,
and alpha is suppressed. When you close your eyes, the visual cortex
has no input, and a synchronous wave develops — like thousands of
neurons humming in unison.

This is the classic signal our models should exploit. In research-grade
EEG, closing your eyes can increase alpha power 2 to 5 times. In OUR
dataset, the increase is only about 13% — much weaker. Why? Cheaper
hardware and the subject wasn't instructed to sustain eye closure, so
they may have just been blinking.

PART 3 — DATA CLEANING
===============================================================================

--- 3.1 WHAT'S A SPIKE / ARTIFACT? ---

A spike is a sudden, large deviation in the signal that is NOT brain
activity.

Causes include:
- An electrode briefly loses skin contact → huge voltage jump
- The subject moves their head → cable yanks → voltage pulse
- The subject blinks → muscles near the eye produce a strong signal
- A cell phone rings nearby → electromagnetic interference

Our dataset has spikes up to 715,897 µV. Real brain activity is only
about 50 µV. So spikes are ~14,000 times larger than the signal we
want. We MUST remove them, otherwise every model will just learn the
spikes and ignore the brain.


--- 3.2 WHAT'S THE IQR METHOD? ---

IQR stands for Interquartile Range. It's a way to find outliers.

Step by step:
1. Line up all the values of a channel from smallest to largest.
2. Find Q1 — the value such that 25% of samples are below it.
3. Find Q3 — the value such that 75% of samples are below it.
4. IQR = Q3 - Q1. This is the "middle width" of the data.
5. Reject any sample that is further than 3 × IQR from the median.

Why 3 and not the textbook 1.5? Because EEG has legitimate high-amplitude
events (alpha bursts, sleep spindles) that we want to KEEP. 1.5 would
throw them away. 3.0 keeps them and only removes clear hardware
failures.

Row-wise: if ANY of the 14 channels flags a row, we drop the whole row.
This keeps the channels aligned in time.


--- 3.3 WHAT'S A FILTER? ---

A filter is a mathematical operation that REMOVES certain frequencies
from a signal while KEEPING others.

Real-world analogy: your car radio has a filter. It lets only the
frequency of your chosen station pass through and blocks all others.

Types of filter:
- Low-pass: keeps low frequencies, removes high (e.g., smoothing)
- High-pass: keeps high, removes low (e.g., removing DC drift)
- Bandpass: keeps a middle range, removes both extremes
- Bandstop / notch: removes a specific narrow range (e.g., 50 Hz hum)

We use a BANDPASS filter from 0.5 to 45 Hz. This removes:
- Below 0.5 Hz: very slow drift (not neural)
- Above 45 Hz: power-line hum (50 Hz in Europe), muscle noise


--- 3.4 WHAT'S A BUTTERWORTH FILTER? ---

Butterworth is a specific DESIGN of filter. It's named after Stephen
Butterworth, who invented it in 1930.

Key property: maximally flat passband. Inside the frequencies you want
to keep, the filter does not distort their amplitude. A Butterworth
filter "leaves alone" the good frequencies very faithfully.

The trade-off: the transition between keep and remove is GENTLE. A
Butterworth doesn't switch from "passing" to "blocking" abruptly.

Alternative filters:
- Chebyshev: sharper transition, but RIPPLES in the passband
- Elliptic: sharpest, but ripples in both keep and remove regions

For EEG, amplitude fidelity matters more than sharpness, so
Butterworth wins.

Filter ORDER: 4th-order is what we used. Higher order = steeper
transition but more ringing in transient responses. 4th is a good
compromise.


--- 3.5 WHAT'S ZERO-PHASE FILTERING? ---

Any filter introduces a "delay" in the signal — output comes out
slightly shifted in time relative to input. This is called phase
distortion.

Zero-phase filtering eliminates this. How? Run the signal through the
filter forward, then reverse it and run it backward through the same
filter. The two passes cancel each other's delays. In Python's scipy
this is done with `scipy.signal.filtfilt`.

For EEG, phase distortion would misalign events across channels, which
would confuse models. Zero-phase keeps everything in sync.


--- 3.6 WHAT'S LOG-NORMALIZATION? ---

Log-normalization is a mathematical transformation: replace each value
x with log(1 + |x|), preserving the sign.

Why would you do this? If your data has heavy tails — many extreme
values — the log compresses large values and expands small ones.
Result: a more Gaussian (bell-shaped) distribution, which many models
prefer.

BUT — and this is what we discovered — log is not always helpful. If
your data is already symmetric around zero (like bandpass-filtered
EEG), the log artificially splits the distribution into two clumps
and INCREASES skewness. We tested it, measured worse skewness on all
14 channels, and rejected it.

Lesson: always measure before and after a transformation. Don't apply
transforms reflexively.


--- 3.7 WHAT'S SKEWNESS AND KURTOSIS? ---

These are numerical descriptions of a distribution's shape, beyond
just mean and variance.

SKEWNESS: how asymmetric is the distribution?
- Skewness 0 = perfectly symmetric (like a normal bell curve)
- Positive skewness = long tail to the right (rare large values)
- Negative skewness = long tail to the left (rare small values)

KURTOSIS: how heavy are the tails?
- Kurtosis 3 = normal distribution (reference point)
- Kurtosis > 3 = heavy tails (more outliers than normal)
- Kurtosis < 3 = light tails (fewer outliers than normal)

Raw EEG had skewness up to 78 and kurtosis up to 6,000 — wildly
non-normal, hardware-dominated. After preprocessing, skewness fell
below 2 and kurtosis below 12 on all channels — much closer to normal.

PART 4 — FEATURE ENGINEERING
===============================================================================

--- 4.1 WHAT'S A FEATURE? ---

A feature is a number computed from raw data that you think might be
useful for prediction.

For EEG:
- Raw values at each sample are features (we have 14 per sample)
- But you can also compute MORE features: rolling averages, band
  powers, asymmetry between left and right, etc.

Why engineer features? Because a simple ML model works better with
carefully-chosen features than with raw data. Deep learning can learn
features automatically, but for small datasets, hand-crafted features
often win.

Our total feature count: 116 (14 raw + 14 rolling means + 14 rolling
stds + 70 FFT features + 4 band ratios).


--- 4.2 WHAT'S HEMISPHERIC ASYMMETRY? ---

Your brain has two hemispheres — left and right. They look similar
but do different things (left is typically more verbal, right more
spatial).

Hemispheric asymmetry is the DIFFERENCE between left and right
channels. For each of 7 symmetric electrode pairs, we compute:

  asymmetry = left_channel - right_channel

Why? Research shows that frontal alpha asymmetry correlates with
emotional state, and disrupted symmetry signals fatigue.

We tested whether each of our 7 asymmetry features differs between
eye-open and eye-closed states using a t-test. Only 2 pairs showed
significant difference: F7-F8 (lateral frontal) and P7-P8 (parietal).

We kept all 7 features anyway. Even non-significant features can help
a model via interactions with other features.


--- 4.3 WHAT'S BAND POWER? ---

For each of the 5 EEG bands (delta, theta, alpha, beta, gamma), we
compute how much energy is in that frequency range.

Computation: run Welch's PSD (see 6.3), then integrate the power
within the band's frequency boundaries.

For each sample, we get 5 band powers per channel — a direct measure
of "how much delta-like activity", "how much alpha", etc.

The most important band power for eye state: ALPHA. When eyes close,
alpha power increases (Berger effect). We measured this increase and
compared to open-eye state. Ratio = 1.13 (13% increase). Literature
says 2-5x.


--- 4.4 WHAT'S A P-VALUE? ---

A p-value tells you "how likely is this result if nothing interesting
is going on?"

Example: you flip a coin 100 times and get 62 heads. Is this a fair
coin?
- If the coin IS fair, the probability of seeing 62 or more heads
  by chance is about 0.01.
- That 0.01 is the p-value.

Interpretation:
- p < 0.05: "unlikely to be a coincidence" → we usually call it
  statistically significant
- p > 0.05: "could be coincidence" → not significant

For our asymmetry test: F7-F8 had p = 0.018 < 0.05 (significant).
AF3-AF4 had p = 0.243 > 0.05 (not significant).

Caveat: p-values are often misunderstood. They do NOT tell you how
big or important an effect is — only whether it's likely to be real.

PART 5 — EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS (EDA)
===============================================================================

--- 5.1 WHAT'S A BOX PLOT? ---

A box plot summarizes a distribution with 5 numbers:
- Minimum (or Q1 - 1.5 × IQR, whichever is smaller)
- Q1 (25th percentile)
- Median (middle value)
- Q3 (75th percentile)
- Maximum (or Q3 + 1.5 × IQR)

The box spans Q1 to Q3. The line inside the box is the median. The
whiskers reach out to the min/max. Points beyond are plotted as
outliers.

Why use box plots? To compare many distributions side-by-side quickly.
In our case, comparing 14 channels before and after preprocessing
shows dramatically how dynamic range shrunk from ±10^5 µV to ±25 µV.


--- 5.2 WHAT'S A HISTOGRAM? ---

A histogram divides the range of values into BINS (buckets) and
counts how many samples fall into each bin. The height of each bar
is the count.

Histograms show the SHAPE of a distribution — is it bell-shaped
(normal), bimodal (two peaks), skewed, uniform?

We overlay two histograms: blue for eyes open, red for eyes closed.
If one shifts or widens relative to the other, that channel is
discriminative. In our data, the two histograms overlap 85-95% on
most channels → weak single-channel discrimination.


--- 5.3 WHAT'S A VIOLIN PLOT? ---

A violin plot is a box plot PLUS a density estimate, mirrored on
both sides so it looks like a violin shape.

The width of the violin at any height tells you how many samples
have that value. Thus a violin shows:
- Where most of the mass is (wide parts)
- Whether there are multiple peaks (bimodality)
- Whether tails are heavy or light

We use violins to check: are our distributions UNIMODAL? If yes,
linear classifiers are a reasonable choice. If bimodal, we might
need non-linear boundaries. Our data is unimodal — linear models
are fine.


--- 5.4 WHAT'S CORRELATION? ---

Correlation measures how two variables MOVE TOGETHER.

Pearson correlation (the most common) ranges from -1 to +1:
- +1 = they move perfectly together (when one goes up, so does the other)
- 0 = independent
- -1 = perfectly opposite (when one goes up, the other goes down)

Example: if two EEG channels are measuring similar brain regions,
their signals will rise and fall together → high positive correlation.


--- 5.5 WHAT'S COLLINEARITY? ---

Collinearity is when multiple features are strongly correlated with
each other. Problem: they contain REDUNDANT information.

Why is this a problem?
- Linear models (Logistic Regression, LDA) can become numerically
  unstable. The coefficients start bouncing around because the model
  can't decide which of the correlated features to use.
- Tree models (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting) handle it fine.
- Neural networks handle it fine.

Remedies:
- Drop one of the correlated features
- Use regularisation (L2 / Ridge) to constrain coefficients
- Use PCA to combine correlated features

Our case: F7-F8 have correlation 0.87 even after cleaning. But this
is PHYSIOLOGICAL (the hemispheres naturally co-activate), not
redundant. We keep both and use L2 regularisation on linear models.


--- 5.6 WHAT'S A HEATMAP? ---

A heatmap is a 2D grid where each cell is colored according to its
value. For correlations, we make a 14×14 grid where row i, column j
shows the correlation between channel i and channel j.

Colour scale: red = high correlation, blue = negative, white = zero.

Reading our heatmap: the diagonal is always red (a channel perfectly
correlates with itself). The off-diagonal entries tell us about
coupling between different channels.

PART 6 — FREQUENCY ANALYSIS
===============================================================================

--- 6.1 WHAT'S A SIGNAL IN TIME VS FREQUENCY? ---

A signal in TIME: a graph of value vs time (e.g., voltage over
milliseconds).

A signal in FREQUENCY: a graph of energy at each frequency (e.g.,
how much 10 Hz, 20 Hz, 30 Hz is in the signal).

Both describe the same signal — just from different perspectives.

Analogy: a piano chord contains multiple notes (frequencies) played
at once. The TIME view shows you the wiggle of the air pressure.
The FREQUENCY view tells you "this chord has C at this intensity,
E at this intensity, G at this intensity."

For EEG, the frequency view is MORE INFORMATIVE than the time view,
because brain states are defined by which frequencies are strong.


--- 6.2 WHAT'S THE FFT? ---

FFT = Fast Fourier Transform.

Fourier discovered (in 1822) that any signal can be written as a sum
of sine waves at different frequencies. The Fourier Transform tells
you the AMPLITUDE and PHASE of each frequency.

"Fast" Fourier Transform (Cooley-Tukey, 1965) is an algorithm that
computes this efficiently. Without it, computing spectra would be
impractical.

For a signal of length N:
- Naive computation: N² operations
- FFT: N × log(N) operations

For N = 1024, that's 1 million vs 10,000 operations — 100× speedup.

Output of FFT: for each frequency bin, a complex number whose magnitude
is the amplitude and whose angle is the phase.


--- 6.3 WHAT'S PSD AND WELCH'S METHOD? ---

PSD = Power Spectral Density. Basically, the amplitude of FFT squared
gives you the POWER at each frequency.

Problem: a single FFT on a short noisy signal is itself noisy. The
estimate has high variance.

Welch's method (1967) fixes this:
1. Break the signal into overlapping chunks (say, 50% overlap)
2. Multiply each chunk by a window function (Hann) to reduce edge
   effects
3. Compute FFT of each chunk, square to get power
4. Average across all chunks

Result: a smooth, reliable PSD with lower variance than a single FFT.

We use Welch's PSD for computing band powers and for visualising
spectral differences between eye-open and eye-closed.


--- 6.4 WHAT'S NYQUIST? ---

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem (1949): to faithfully represent
a signal containing frequencies up to F, you must sample at a rate of
at least 2F.

Intuitively: if you sample too slowly, fast oscillations look slow
(the classic wagon-wheel illusion in old Western movies).

Our sampling rate: 128 Hz. So our Nyquist frequency is 64 Hz. We
cannot reliably measure anything above 64 Hz.

Gamma band extends to 100 Hz in theory, but we can only see up to
64 Hz. And since 50 Hz line noise is strong, we filter out above 45 Hz
anyway. So our effective gamma range is just 30-45 Hz.


--- 6.5 WHAT'S 1/F PINK NOISE? ---

Many natural signals — including brain activity — have a spectral
shape where power is INVERSELY PROPORTIONAL to frequency. This is
called "1/f" or "pink noise".

Compared to white noise (equal power at all frequencies):
- Low frequencies have MORE power in 1/f noise
- High frequencies have LESS power

This 1/f shape is the baseline of cortical activity. Interesting
brain states show DEVIATIONS from 1/f — peaks in alpha, peaks in
beta, etc.

Our data shows the expected 1/f shape, confirming preprocessing
preserved physiological structure. Small alpha bumps on occipital
channels are the only deviation — and that bump IS the Berger effect.

PART 7 — DIMENSIONALITY REDUCTION
===============================================================================

--- 7.1 WHAT DOES "DIMENSIONALITY" MEAN? ---

Each feature in your data is a DIMENSION. If you have 116 features
per sample, your data lives in 116-dimensional space.

You cannot visualise 116D directly. Humans can barely grasp 3D. So
to SEE class structure, you project to 1, 2, or 3 dimensions.

Good projection = keeps important differences between classes.
Bad projection = loses class structure.


--- 7.2 WHAT'S LDA? ---

LDA = Linear Discriminant Analysis. A SUPERVISED method — it uses
class labels.

Goal: find the direction (1D axis) that maximally separates the
classes. Formally: maximise the ratio of between-class variance to
within-class variance (Fisher's criterion).

For binary classification, LDA produces exactly 1 axis. Projecting
data onto this axis gives each sample a single number. If the two
classes are separable by a line, their projections will be mostly
separated.

Our result: LDA achieved Silhouette = 0.156. Modest but real
separation. This is why LINEAR MODELS (Logistic Regression, LDA)
work well for us — the classes are linearly distinguishable, with
overlap.


--- 7.3 WHAT'S T-SNE? ---

t-SNE = t-distributed Stochastic Neighbour Embedding. An UNSUPERVISED
method — it does NOT use labels.

Goal: preserve LOCAL similarity between points. Points close together
in 116D should be close in 2D. Far-apart points can be arranged
arbitrarily.

Algorithm:
1. Compute pairwise similarities in high-D using Gaussian kernels
2. Compute pairwise similarities in low-D using heavy-tailed
   t-distributions (prevents crowding)
3. Move points in low-D to minimise KL divergence between the two
   sets of similarities

Parameters:
- Perplexity: roughly "how many neighbours each point cares about"
  (we use 30)
- Learning rate: step size in the optimisation

IMPORTANT caveats:
- Results depend on random seed and perplexity
- Cluster SIZES in a t-SNE plot are NOT meaningful
- Distances BETWEEN clusters are NOT meaningful
- Use t-SNE for visualisation only, not analysis


--- 7.4 WHAT'S UMAP? ---

UMAP = Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection. Published in
2018 as an improved t-SNE.

Advantages over t-SNE:
- Preserves BOTH local AND global structure
- Inter-cluster distances are meaningful
- Deterministic given a seed (reproducible)
- Scales to millions of points

Algorithm (simplified):
1. Build a k-nearest-neighbour graph in high-D
2. Assign fuzzy probabilities to edges
3. Optimise low-D layout to best preserve the high-D topology

Our result: UMAP silhouette 0.065 (slightly better than t-SNE's
0.054). Confirms weak class structure in feature space — supervision
is essential.


--- 7.5 WHAT ARE CLUSTERING METRICS? ---

To compare dimensionality reduction methods numerically, we use three
metrics:

SILHOUETTE SCORE (higher better, range -1 to +1):
For each point, compute:
- a = average distance to other points in same cluster
- b = average distance to points in nearest other cluster
- silhouette = (b - a) / max(a, b)
Average across all points. Positive = separated, negative = overlapping.

DAVIES-BOULDIN INDEX (lower better, >= 0):
Average similarity of each cluster to its nearest neighbour cluster,
where similarity is a ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-
cluster separation.

CALINSKI-HARABASZ INDEX (higher better, >= 0):
Ratio of between-cluster dispersion to within-cluster dispersion.

Our results: LDA wins ALL three. Supervised dominates unsupervised
when classes exist.

PART 8 — TRAINING MODELS
===============================================================================

--- 8.1 WHAT'S TRAIN / VALIDATION / TEST? ---

When training a model, we split data into three parts:

TRAIN (usually 60-80%): the model sees these examples and LEARNS
from them.

VALIDATION (usually 10-20%): used to tune hyperparameters (like
which model, which settings). The model DOES NOT learn from these,
but we pick the best version based on how well it performs here.

TEST (usually 10-20%): the FINAL evaluation. Never touched during
training or tuning. Gives an honest estimate of real-world
performance.

Our splits: 60/20/20, 70/15/15, 80/10/10. All CHRONOLOGICAL (earliest
data in train, latest in test).

Why chronological? Because our dataset has CONCEPT DRIFT (see 8.6).
Random shuffling would leak future information into the past and
make results look artificially good.


--- 8.2 WHAT'S CLASS IMBALANCE? ---

Class imbalance is when classes have different sizes. Our data: 55.1%
open, 44.9% closed. Mild imbalance.

Severe imbalance (e.g., 99% class A, 1% class B) is a big problem:
the model can achieve 99% accuracy by always predicting A, without
learning anything. Mild imbalance is less severe but still affects
decision thresholds.

Mitigations:
- CLASS WEIGHTS: during training, multiply the loss for minority-class
  samples by a factor so they count more. Formula: w_c = N / (2 × N_c)
- RESAMPLING: oversample minority (SMOTE) or undersample majority.
  Not needed for our mild imbalance.
- THRESHOLD TUNING: after training, search for the decision threshold
  that maximises the metric you care about.


--- 8.3 WHAT'S MACRO-F1? ---

F1 is a metric that combines precision and recall for one class.

For a class:
- PRECISION = (true positives) / (true positives + false positives)
  "When I predicted this class, how often was I right?"
- RECALL = (true positives) / (true positives + false negatives)
  "Of all actual samples of this class, how many did I catch?"
- F1 = 2 × precision × recall / (precision + recall)
  The harmonic mean. High F1 requires BOTH high precision AND high
  recall.

MACRO-F1 = average of F1 across all classes.

  Macro-F1 = (F1_class0 + F1_class1) / 2

Why Macro-F1? Unlike accuracy, which can be dominated by the majority
class, Macro-F1 gives equal weight to each class. Critical for
imbalanced data. Our primary metric.


--- 8.4 WHAT'S AUC-ROC? ---

ROC = Receiver Operating Characteristic curve.
AUC = Area Under the Curve.

How it works:
1. For each possible decision threshold, compute True Positive Rate
   (TPR) and False Positive Rate (FPR).
2. Plot TPR vs FPR as threshold varies — this is the ROC curve.
3. Area under this curve (AUC) summarises the whole thing in one
   number.

Interpretation:
- AUC = 1.0: perfect classifier
- AUC = 0.5: random guessing
- AUC < 0.5: WORSE than random (inverted ranking)

AUC is THRESHOLD-INDEPENDENT. It measures how well the model RANKS
positives above negatives, regardless of where you draw the decision
line.

In our results, several models have AUC around 0.3-0.4 (worse than
random). This is because CONCEPT DRIFT inverts the ranking between
training and test.


--- 8.5 WHAT'S A DECISION THRESHOLD? ---

Classifiers output a PROBABILITY (0 to 1). To make a hard decision
(class 0 vs 1), you pick a THRESHOLD.

Default threshold is usually 0.5: if P ≥ 0.5, predict class 1; else
class 0.

But you can CHOOSE a different threshold based on what metric you
want to optimise:
- Lower threshold (e.g., 0.3): more class-1 predictions → higher
  recall, lower precision
- Higher threshold (e.g., 0.7): fewer class-1 predictions → higher
  precision, lower recall

We tune the threshold on the validation set to maximise Macro-F1 for
each model. Our optimal thresholds range from 0.53 to 0.95 across
models and splits — quite variable, indicating instability.


--- 8.6 WHAT'S CONCEPT DRIFT? ---

Concept drift is when the statistical properties of the data change
over TIME.

Our case: the proportion of eyes-closed samples varies across
quartiles of the recording:
- Q1: 49.8% closed
- Q2: 62.3% closed
- Q3: 41.5% closed
- Q4: 24.2% closed
- Last 15%: only 8.1% closed

So if we train on the first 70% and test on the last 15%, the test
set's class balance is radically different. A model that predicts
"mostly closed" (appropriate for training data) will over-predict
closed on test data where most samples are actually open.

This is why our test AUC often flips below 0.5 — the ranking from
training no longer applies to test. Accuracy can stay high (models
still get most of the test right, but only because they default to
the majority class "open" in the drifted test distribution).

Remedies:
- Use Macro-F1 instead of accuracy (more robust to class shift)
- Tune thresholds on validation data
- Adapt the model online (not done here, but possible)
- Collect data that covers the full distribution of expected test
  conditions

PART 9 — MACHINE LEARNING ALGORITHMS
===============================================================================

--- 9.1 WHAT'S LOGISTIC REGRESSION? ---

Despite the name, it's a CLASSIFIER (not regression).

Model: P(y=1 | x) = sigmoid(w·x + b)

Where:
- x is the feature vector
- w is a weight vector (learned)
- b is a bias (learned)
- sigmoid squashes any real number into [0,1]

Sigmoid function: sigmoid(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(-z))
- sigmoid(0) = 0.5
- sigmoid(+∞) → 1
- sigmoid(-∞) → 0

Training: find w and b that minimise binary cross-entropy loss. This
is a CONVEX optimisation (there's a single global minimum). Efficient
solvers like L-BFGS find it quickly.

Properties:
- FAST to train and predict
- INTERPRETABLE: each weight tells you how much that feature matters
- Produces well-CALIBRATED probabilities
- Only learns LINEAR decision boundaries

Our hyperparameters:
- L2 penalty (Ridge regularisation)
- C = 1.0 (inverse regularisation strength)
- Balanced class weights

Result: BEST ML model by mean Macro-F1 (0.4665). Most stable across
splits. The simple model wins.


--- 9.2 WHAT'S SVM? ---

SVM = Support Vector Machine. A maximum-margin classifier.

Idea: find the hyperplane (a boundary that separates classes) that
maximises the DISTANCE to the nearest training points on each side.
Those nearest points are called "support vectors".

For linearly separable data: one such hyperplane exists.
For non-separable data: allow some violations (soft margin) but
penalise them. The C parameter controls this trade-off.

For non-linear separation: use the KERNEL TRICK (see 9.3).

Our use: SVM with RBF kernel. Mean Macro-F1 = 0.4233. Rank 7.

Why not the best here: RBF kernel fits local curvature of training
data. When concept drift shifts the test distribution, learned
curvature doesn't apply. High variance across splits.


--- 9.3 WHAT'S A KERNEL? ---

A kernel is a similarity function: K(x, y) returns a number saying
how similar x and y are.

The "kernel trick": if you can compute K(x, y) without explicitly
mapping to a high-dimensional space, you can run linear algorithms
in that high-D space implicitly. This lets linear models (like SVM)
learn non-linear decision boundaries.

RBF (Radial Basis Function / Gaussian) kernel:

  K(x, y) = exp(-gamma × ||x - y||²)

Properties:
- Output ranges 0 to 1
- = 1 when x = y (identical)
- → 0 as x and y get far apart
- gamma controls how quickly similarity drops with distance

The RBF kernel corresponds to an infinite-dimensional feature mapping.
An SVM with RBF can, in principle, separate ANY dataset. In practice,
it can overfit.


--- 9.4 WHAT'S RANDOM FOREST? ---

Random Forest is an ENSEMBLE of decision trees.

DECISION TREE: a model that asks yes/no questions about features to
partition the data. "Is F7 > 10?" → branch one way or the other →
another question → eventually reach a leaf with a prediction.

Single trees are easy to overfit. Random Forest fights this with two
randomisations:

1. BAGGING (Bootstrap Aggregation): each tree is trained on a random
   bootstrap sample (with replacement) of rows.
2. FEATURE RANDOMNESS: at each node, only a random subset of √p
   features is considered for splitting.

Prediction: majority vote across all trees.

Why it works: bagging reduces variance. Feature randomness makes
trees DECORRELATED. Multiple uncorrelated models averaged together
perform better than any single one.

Bonus: Random Forest gives FEATURE IMPORTANCE — how much each feature
helped reduce impurity across trees. Our RF revealed F7, F8, AF3, AF4,
FC5 as top features (lateral frontal channels — they pick up eye-blink
signals).

Our config: 200 trees, max depth None, Gini impurity, balanced
weights. Mean Macro-F1 = 0.4311. Rank 6.


--- 9.5 WHAT'S GRADIENT BOOSTING? ---

Gradient Boosting is another tree ensemble, but SEQUENTIAL (not
parallel like Random Forest).

Idea: at each step, train a new tree that corrects the MISTAKES of
the current ensemble. Keep adding trees until you have enough.

Math:
  F_m(x) = F_{m-1}(x) + η × h_m(x)

Where:
- F_m is the ensemble after m trees
- h_m is the new tree at step m
- η is the learning rate (shrinkage, typically 0.1)
- h_m fits the NEGATIVE GRADIENT of the loss at F_{m-1}

"Gradient" because each tree moves the ensemble in the direction of
steepest loss reduction.

Versus Random Forest:
- RF: independent trees, parallel training, averaged predictions
- GB: dependent trees, sequential training, additive predictions

Our config: 200 trees, lr = 0.1, max depth 5, balanced weights.
Mean Macro-F1 = 0.4723. Rank 4.

Best ML model for STABILITY (lowest variance across splits).


--- 9.6 WHAT'S XGBOOST? ---

XGBoost = eXtreme Gradient Boosting. Published in 2016, became
famous for winning Kaggle competitions.

It's basically Gradient Boosting with optimisations:
- L1 AND L2 regularisation on leaf weights
- HISTOGRAM-BASED split finding (bin features first, faster)
- Handles MISSING VALUES natively
- Parallel tree construction (not sequential within a single tree)
- Early stopping
- scale_pos_weight for imbalance

In theory, better than vanilla GB. In practice, on our small dataset,
default regularisation is TOO aggressive and under-fits the weak
signal. Mean Macro-F1 = 0.4363. Rank 8.

Would benefit from hyperparameter search. But shows XGBoost is not
automatically better.


--- 9.7 WHAT'S REGULARISATION? ---

Regularisation is a technique to prevent OVERFITTING.

Without regularisation: model tries to fit training data perfectly,
including noise. Poor performance on new data.

With regularisation: add a penalty to the loss function that
discourages large weights.

Two common forms:
- L1 (Lasso): penalty proportional to |w| → produces SPARSE solutions
  (many weights exactly zero) → implicit feature selection
- L2 (Ridge): penalty proportional to w² → shrinks all weights
  toward zero but rarely to exactly zero

Effect: smaller weights = smoother decision boundary = less overfitting.

The strength is controlled by a hyperparameter (alpha, lambda, or
1/C depending on the library). Tuned via cross-validation.

Our Logistic Regression uses L2 with C = 1.0. Our linear SVM also
uses L2 implicitly through its hinge loss + margin formulation.

PART 10 — DEEP LEARNING CONCEPTS
===============================================================================

--- 10.1 WHAT'S A NEURAL NETWORK? ---

A neural network is a model made of LAYERS of simple computations.

Basic unit (neuron): takes inputs, multiplies each by a weight, adds a
bias, applies a non-linear "activation function", produces an output.

  output = activation(w1 × x1 + w2 × x2 + ... + wn × xn + b)

Stack many neurons in a layer. Stack many layers. Connect them.
That's a neural network. Millions of weights, all learned from data.

"Deep" learning = neural networks with MANY layers (more than 2-3).

Why deep? Each layer can learn progressively more abstract features.
Layer 1 might detect edges. Layer 2 might detect shapes. Layer 3
might detect objects. This HIERARCHY is what makes deep networks so
powerful.


--- 10.2 WHAT'S AN ACTIVATION FUNCTION? ---

A non-linear function applied after each layer. Without it, stacked
layers collapse into one big linear layer (no more expressive than
logistic regression).

Common activations:
- SIGMOID: σ(z) = 1/(1+e^-z). Output [0,1]. Used mostly at the final
  layer for binary classification.
- TANH: (e^z - e^-z)/(e^z + e^-z). Output [-1,+1]. Zero-centered.
- RELU: max(0, z). Simple, fast, works great in deep networks.
- ELU: z if z > 0; α(e^z - 1) otherwise. Smooth version of ReLU.
  Used in EEGNet.
- GELU: Gaussian Error Linear Unit. Used in Transformers.

Activation determines which signals get "passed through" and which
get suppressed. Non-linearity is why networks can learn complex
functions.


--- 10.3 WHAT'S BACKPROPAGATION? ---

Backprop is the algorithm used to TRAIN neural networks.

Forward pass: feed input through network, get prediction.
Compute loss (how wrong the prediction was).
Backward pass: compute gradient of loss with respect to EACH weight.
Update: adjust each weight in direction that DECREASES loss.

The gradient is computed by the CHAIN RULE from calculus. Each
layer "passes back" the gradient to the previous layer.

Without backprop, we couldn't train deep networks. It's the key
algorithm from the 1970s-80s that enabled modern deep learning.


--- 10.4 WHAT'S A LOSS FUNCTION? ---

A loss function measures how wrong the model's predictions are.
Training means MINIMISING this loss.

Common losses:
- Mean Squared Error (MSE): for regression. (prediction - truth)².
- Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE): for binary classification.
   L = -[y log(p) + (1-y) log(1-p)]
  When y=1, L = -log(p). Drives p toward 1.
  When y=0, L = -log(1-p). Drives p toward 0.

In our project, we use WEIGHTED BCE to handle class imbalance. Each
sample contributes w_c × BCE, where w_c = N/(2*N_c) — the inverse
class frequency.


--- 10.5 WHAT'S AN OPTIMIZER? ---

An optimizer is the algorithm that UPDATES weights based on gradients.

Simplest: Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). New weight = old weight
minus learning_rate × gradient.

Better: SGD with momentum. Accumulate gradients over time (like a
ball rolling downhill). Faster convergence.

Adam (2014): adaptive learning rates per parameter. Tracks running
averages of gradient and squared gradient. Default choice for most
deep learning.

AdamW (2017): Adam with DECOUPLED weight decay. Slightly better
generalisation than vanilla Adam. This is what we use.

Learning rate of 1e-3 (0.001) is a standard starting point.


--- 10.6 WHAT'S LEARNING RATE? ---

The learning rate controls how big a step you take in the direction
of the gradient.

- Too high: overshoots the minimum, loss oscillates or diverges.
- Too low: training takes forever, may get stuck in local minima.

We use COSINE ANNEALING: start at lr = 1e-3, smoothly decrease
following a cosine curve over all epochs, ending at 0. This lets
us take big steps early and fine-tune with small steps at the end.


--- 10.7 WHAT'S DROPOUT? ---

Dropout is a regularisation technique for neural networks.

During training: randomly set a fraction p of neurons' outputs to 0
in each layer, each batch. Typical p = 0.3 to 0.5.

Why it helps: forces the network to not rely too heavily on any
single neuron. Creates an implicit ENSEMBLE of sub-networks.

During inference (test time): all neurons are active, outputs are
scaled to match training behaviour.

Our DL models use dropout 0.3 in their final layers.


--- 10.8 WHAT'S OVERFITTING? ---

Overfitting = the model learns the training data TOO WELL, including
noise and quirks. It then fails on new data.

Telltale signs:
- Training loss keeps going down
- Validation loss starts going UP
- Big gap between train and validation performance

Our LSTM and CNN-LSTM overfit badly. Train loss reached 0.04 while
CV loss rose to 2.86. The networks had too many parameters (200k,
150k) for our ~9,500 training samples.

Remedies:
- More data
- Smaller model (fewer parameters)
- Regularisation (L2, dropout)
- Early stopping
- Data augmentation

Our EEGNet (only 400 params) was TOO SMALL to overfit. That's why it
generalised best.


--- 10.9 WHAT'S A LOSS CURVE? ---

A loss curve plots loss vs. training epoch. Usually TWO curves:
train loss and validation loss.

6 patterns to recognise:

1. HEALTHY FIT: both decrease, plateau near each other. Small gap.

2. OVERFITTING: train decreases, CV rises after some epoch. Gap
   grows. Model is memorising.

3. UNDERFITTING: both plateau at high values. Model lacks capacity
   or signal is too weak.

4. LR TOO HIGH: losses oscillate or diverge. Reduce learning rate.

5. CLASS COLLAPSE: loss drops briefly, flatlines. All predictions =
   majority class.

6. PATHOLOGICAL NOISY CV: CV oscillates wildly while train smooths.
   CV set too small or model too variable.

Learn to read these patterns — they tell you what's wrong and what
to try next.

PART 11 — DEEP LEARNING ARCHITECTURES
===============================================================================

--- 11.1 WHAT'S A CNN? ---

CNN = Convolutional Neural Network.

Built around the CONVOLUTION operation (11.2). Originally designed
for images (where the same feature can appear anywhere in the
image), but also works great for time series (where the same pattern
can appear at any time).

Structure:
- Convolutional layers extract LOCAL features
- Pooling layers reduce resolution
- Final dense layer(s) make the classification decision

Why CNNs work:
- TRANSLATION INVARIANCE: a pattern is detected no matter where it
  appears
- PARAMETER SHARING: the same filter is applied everywhere, so fewer
  parameters than fully-connected
- HIERARCHICAL: early layers detect simple patterns, later layers
  detect complex ones


--- 11.2 WHAT'S A CONVOLUTION? ---

A convolution slides a small FILTER (also called a kernel) across the
input, computing a weighted sum at each position.

For 1D (time series):
  output[t] = sum over k of (filter[k] × input[t+k])

The filter has a fixed number of weights (e.g., 5). These weights
are LEARNED during training.

Each filter learns to detect one pattern. Multiple filters (say, 32)
detect 32 different patterns.

Analogy: a filter is like a template. Sliding it along the signal
measures how much each location "looks like" the template.

In CNN-LSTM, we use Conv1D with kernel size 5, covering about 40 ms
of signal. Each filter is a learned time-frequency pattern.


--- 11.3 WHAT'S AN LSTM? ---

LSTM = Long Short-Term Memory. A type of Recurrent Neural Network
(RNN).

RNNs process sequences one step at a time, maintaining a HIDDEN STATE
that summarises past information.

Problem with vanilla RNNs: gradients vanish over long sequences, so
the network can't remember distant events.

LSTM solution: use a CELL STATE that flows through time with minimal
disturbance, regulated by three GATES:
- Forget gate: decides what to erase from the cell
- Input gate: decides what to add
- Output gate: decides what to output

Equations:
  f_t = sigmoid(W_f · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f)    forget gate
  i_t = sigmoid(W_i · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i)    input gate
  C_t = f_t × C_{t-1} + i_t × tanh(...)        update cell
  o_t = sigmoid(W_o · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o)    output gate
  h_t = o_t × tanh(C_t)                        hidden output

BIDIRECTIONAL LSTM: runs two LSTMs — one forward, one backward —
combines their outputs. Uses both past AND future context. Useful
for classification tasks where the full sequence is known.

Our LSTM: 2 stacked BiLSTM layers, 64 hidden units each. 200k
parameters. Overfit badly on our small dataset.


--- 11.4 WHAT'S A CNN-LSTM? ---

A hybrid: CNN layers first, then LSTM layers.

Idea:
- CNN extracts LOCAL features (e.g., short oscillatory bursts)
- LSTM models the SEQUENCE of those features

Standard for speech recognition (CNN → LSTM → CTC) and many biosignal
tasks.

Our CNN-LSTM:
- Conv1D(32 filters, kernel 5) → ReLU → MaxPool
- Conv1D(64 filters, kernel 3) → ReLU → MaxPool
- BiLSTM(64 hidden) → Dropout → Dense

~150k parameters. Macro-F1 = 0.4837. Rank 2 overall. Jumping from
plain LSTM (0.42) shows that local feature extraction helps
significantly.


--- 11.5 WHAT'S A TRANSFORMER? ---

Transformer = a neural network architecture based on ATTENTION,
published in 2017 ("Attention Is All You Need").

Core idea: every position in the sequence can directly "attend to"
every other position, without having to propagate information
sequentially like in an RNN.

Advantages:
- Captures LONG-RANGE dependencies easily
- Parallelisable (unlike RNNs, which are sequential)
- Scales well with data size

Basic structure:
- Input is embedded into a vector space
- POSITIONAL ENCODING is added so the model knows token order
- SELF-ATTENTION layer (see 11.6) mixes tokens
- Feedforward layer applies non-linearity per position
- Stack these blocks N times
- Classification head at the end

Used in BERT, GPT, and modern language models.

Our EEG Transformer: 80k params, 3 encoder layers. COLLAPSED on our
small dataset. Attention is data-hungry.


--- 11.6 WHAT'S SELF-ATTENTION? ---

Self-attention lets each position in a sequence look at EVERY OTHER
position and decide what to focus on.

Mechanism:
1. Each token gets transformed into three vectors: Query (Q), Key
   (K), and Value (V). These are learned linear projections.
2. For each token, compute similarity (dot product) between its Q
   and the K of every other token. These become the ATTENTION WEIGHTS.
3. Each token's output is a weighted sum of all V vectors, using the
   attention weights.

Formula:
  Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q·K^T / √d_k) · V

MULTI-HEAD ATTENTION: run attention H times in parallel with
different projections, concatenate results. Allows the model to attend
to different aspects simultaneously.

Typical config in our EEG Transformer: 4 heads, embed dim 32,
feedforward dim 128.

Why it failed here: self-attention requires LOTS of data to learn
useful query/key projections. With only 9,500 training samples and
80k parameters, the model couldn't discover the right attention
patterns.


--- 11.7 WHAT'S EEGNET? ---

EEGNet is a compact CNN designed specifically for EEG, published by
Lawhern et al. in 2018. Uses DEPTHWISE and SEPARABLE CONVOLUTIONS to
keep parameter count extremely low.

Architecture (3 blocks):

Block 1 (temporal filtering):
  Conv2D(kernel=1×64, filters=8) → BatchNorm
  Learns 8 filters in TIME only. Like a learned band-pass for each
  filter.

Block 2 (spatial filtering):
  DepthwiseConv2D(kernel=14×1, depth_mult=2) → BN → ELU →
  AvgPool(1×4) → Dropout
  Learns how to combine channels. DEPTHWISE = one filter per input
  channel, no channel-mixing.

Block 3 (separable conv):
  SeparableConv2D(kernel=1×16) → BN → ELU → AvgPool(1×8) → Dropout
  → Dense(2)

Total parameters: ONLY ~400.

Why it won on our data:
- Too few parameters to overfit
- Inductive bias matches EEG physics
- Temporal filter = learned band-pass
- Spatial filter = learned electrode weighting

EEGNet is the canonical "default choice" for EEG classification on
small/medium datasets.


--- 11.8 WHAT'S DEPTHWISE CONVOLUTION? ---

Standard Conv2D: a filter looks at ALL input channels and produces
ONE output channel. Many filters produce many output channels. Lots
of parameters.

Depthwise Conv2D: each input channel gets its OWN filter. No
channel-mixing. Fewer parameters.

DepthMultiplier D: each input channel can have D filters (still no
cross-channel mixing). Output has D × input_channels.

Separable Conv = Depthwise + Pointwise (1×1) convolution. First
depthwise filters each channel independently, then pointwise mixes
channels. This combo has ~10× fewer parameters than a regular
convolution but nearly equivalent expressive power.

Used in MobileNet (for mobile ML) and EEGNet.


--- 11.9 WHAT'S PATCHTST? ---

PatchTST = Patch-based Time Series Transformer. Published in 2023 by
Nie et al., originally for long-horizon forecasting.

Key innovation: instead of having a token for each TIME STEP (which
would scale badly with sequence length), have a token for each PATCH
(a short window of consecutive time steps).

Our "Lite" version:
- Input (64 samples, 14 channels) → 15 patches of 8 samples each,
  stride 4 (50% overlap)
- Each patch linearly embedded into a 32-dim token
- Add positional encoding, prepend CLS token
- Apply 2 Transformer encoder layers
- Classification head

Why patching helps:
- Attention complexity drops from O(L²) to O((L/P)²) where L is
  sequence length and P is patch size
- Each patch already contains local temporal pattern, easier for
  attention to reason about

Our result: low Macro-F1 (0.28) but near-zero false negatives.
Predicts "closed" aggressively. Good for safety-critical applications
where missing a closed-eye event is catastrophic.


===============================================================================
PART 12 — PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER
===============================================================================

--- 12.1 WHY IS THIS DATASET HARD? ---

Five reasons in order of severity:

1. TEMPORAL CONCEPT DRIFT: the class distribution changes dramatically
   across the recording. Last 15% is only 8% closed.

2. WEAK PHYSIOLOGICAL SIGNAL: alpha ratio closed/open is only 1.13
   versus literature 2-5×. The Berger effect we should be exploiting
   is muted.

3. SMALL DATASET: 9,500-10,500 training samples is small for modern
   deep learning.

4. CONSUMER-GRADE HARDWARE: Emotiv EPOC has lower signal-to-noise
   than research-grade EEG.

5. SINGLE SUBJECT: no cross-subject variation in training. Results
   don't generalise beyond this one recording.


--- 12.2 WHY DID EEGNET WIN? ---

EEGNet won for three reasons:

1. EXTREMELY SMALL (~400 params): physically cannot overfit 9,500
   training samples. Regularisation by architecture.

2. DOMAIN-SPECIFIC INDUCTIVE BIAS: depthwise temporal filters act as
   learned band-passes per channel. Depthwise spatial filters learn
   optimal electrode weightings. This matches EEG physics.

3. NATURALLY CALIBRATED THRESHOLDS: optimal threshold ~0.65, close
   to the ideal 0.5. Means the decision boundary GENERALISES rather
   than being hacked in place by post-hoc tuning.

Key lesson: when data is limited, inductive bias beats brute-force
capacity. 400 parameters with the right structure beat 200,000
without.


--- 12.3 WHY DID TRANSFORMER FAIL? ---

The EEG Transformer collapsed to predicting only the majority class.
Macro-F1 stuck at 0.27 across all 25 epochs. Three reasons:

1. DATA-HUNGRY ARCHITECTURE: Transformers need millions of samples
   to learn useful attention patterns. We had 9,500.

2. NO INDUCTIVE BIAS: unlike CNNs (translation invariance) or RNNs
   (temporal order), Transformers are nearly assumption-free. Without
   data, they can't find structure.

3. WEAK SIGNAL: combined with a weak physiological signal, the
   Transformer defaulted to the "easy solution": predict majority
   class always.

Remedies for future work:
- PRE-TRAIN on large unlabelled EEG corpora (TUH, PhysioNet)
- Add CONVOLUTIONAL FRONT-END to provide inductive bias
- Use STRONGER REGULARISATION
- DATA AUGMENTATION (mixup, time masking, channel masking)


--- 12.4 WHAT WOULD YOU DO DIFFERENTLY? ---

If we had unlimited time and budget:

1. MORE DATA: record from multiple subjects over multiple sessions.
   Aim for cross-subject validation.

2. BETTER HARDWARE: research-grade EEG (gel electrodes, bipolar
   montage) would likely triple the alpha signal strength.

3. HANDLE DRIFT EXPLICITLY: use domain adaptation or online learning
   to adapt to distribution shift at test time.

4. PRE-TRAIN: use self-supervised learning on unlabelled EEG (e.g.,
   contrastive learning, masked prediction) to learn generic
   representations, then fine-tune on our task.

5. ENSEMBLE: combine EEGNet + LogReg + PatchTST with weighted voting.
   Each captures different aspects of the signal.

6. EARLY STOPPING: save the model at the CV-loss minimum, not the
   final epoch.

7. HYPERPARAMETER SEARCH: systematic Bayesian optimisation over key
   hyperparameters for each model.

8. SCORE-BASED CALIBRATION: use isotonic regression or Platt scaling
   to fix probability calibration after threshold tuning.

9. INTERPRETABILITY: compute saliency maps / attention weights on
   the best model to verify it's using physiologically meaningful
   features, not artifacts.

10. REPLICATE ON DIFFERENT DATASETS: test on BCI Competition IV or
    PhysioNet's EEG-MMI dataset to confirm generalisation.
