SLIDE 3 — DATASET: UCI EEG EYE STATE
===============================================================================

The dataset we worked with is the UCI EEG Eye State dataset, published by
Oliver Rösler in 2013.

Here are the key numbers. We have 14,980 samples. They were recorded over
117 seconds of continuous brain activity. The sampling rate is 128 Hz —
meaning 128 measurements per second. And there are 14 EEG channels.

The class balance is mild: 55.1% of samples were recorded with eyes open,
and 44.9% with eyes closed.

The hardware matters a lot here. This data was recorded with an Emotiv
EPOC headset. That is a consumer-grade, wireless, 14-channel device using
saline-soaked felt electrodes — not the research-grade gel electrodes you
would find in a clinical setting. The signal quality is good enough for
basic research, but the signal-to-noise ratio is lower, and as we'll see,
the raw data contains voltage spikes up to 715 thousand microvolts — that
is four orders of magnitude larger than real brain activity.

The labelling was done manually, by watching a synchronised video recording.
The target variable is called eyeDetection, and it's binary: zero for open,
one for closed.

Why does this dataset matter? It is the canonical benchmark for drowsiness
detection and passive brain-computer interfaces. Over 200 peer-reviewed
papers have used it.

SLIDE 4 — ELECTRODE POSITIONING: THE 10-20 SYSTEM
===============================================================================

To understand what each channel measures, we need to talk about how
electrodes are placed on the head.

The international standard is called the 10-20 system. It was adopted in
1958, and it's used universally in EEG research.

The idea is simple. You measure the distance between four anatomical
landmarks — the bridge of the nose, the base of the skull at the back,
and the two points in front of the ears. Then you place electrodes at
ten percent or twenty percent of those distances. That's where the name
comes from.

Every electrode has a name following a convention. The letter tells you
the lobe: F for frontal, C for central, T for temporal, P for parietal,
O for occipital. The number tells you the hemisphere: odd numbers go on
the left, even numbers on the right. Z means midline.

On the head diagram here, you can see our 14 electrodes colour-coded by
region. Six frontal electrodes in red at the top. Two central in orange.
Two temporal in blue on the sides. Two parietal in green. Two occipital
in purple at the back.

The beauty of the 10-20 system is that F7 on one person is anatomically
equivalent to F7 on another person, regardless of head size. That makes
EEG research reproducible across subjects and labs.

SLIDE 5 — ELECTRODE FUNCTIONAL REGIONS
===============================================================================

Now let me tell you what each brain region actually does, and why it
might matter for detecting eye state.

The frontal region has six of our fourteen channels — nearly half. These
electrodes measure executive control, attention, working memory, and
voluntary motor planning. For our task, this is where the brain decides
to close the eyes. It's also where we pick up electrical activity from
eye-blink muscles. We'll see later that the Random Forest model ranks
F7, F8, and AF3 as its most important features.

The central region, with FC5 and FC6, captures motor preparation — the
so-called mu rhythm at 8 to 13 Hz over the sensorimotor cortex.

The temporal region with T7 and T8 handles auditory processing and face
or voice recognition. Less directly relevant for eye state.

Parietal — P7 and P8 — handles spatial attention. Alpha power increases
here when eyes close, contributing to the Berger effect we'll see later.

And the occipital region with O1 and O2 is the primary visual cortex.
Classically, this is where the Berger effect is strongest. But
interestingly — and we'll come back to this — in our data, the occipital
channels rank only mid-table in feature importance. The hardware quality
has shifted the discriminative signal toward the frontal channels.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 6 — TARGET VARIABLE AND CLASS DISTRIBUTION
===============================================================================

This slide has two parts, and the second part is probably the most
important single fact in the entire presentation.

First, the simple class count. 8,257 samples with eyes open, 6,723 with
eyes closed. That is a 55-to-45 split — mild imbalance, which means we
don't need heavy resampling techniques. Simple class weighting will do.

Now here is the crucial observation. If we break the recording into
quartiles by time, look what happens. In the first quartile, about 50%
of samples are closed. In the second quartile, it peaks at 62%. In the
third quartile, it drops to 41%. And in the fourth quartile, only 24%
of samples are closed.

If we zoom into the very last 15% of the recording, only 8% of samples
are eyes-closed. (pause)

This is called temporal concept drift. The statistical distribution of
the target variable changes systematically over the course of the
recording. And it has a massive implication: if we split the data
chronologically — train on the first 70%, test on the last 15% — the
test set has a completely different class distribution from the
training set.

This single fact explains why every model we tested has a strange and
sometimes poor test AUC. The models learn the distribution they saw
during training, and the test distribution is different.

We mitigate this with Macro-F1 as our primary metric, balanced class
weights, and decision thresholds tuned on cross-validation.

SLIDE 7 — PREPROCESSING STEP 1: IQR SPIKE REMOVAL
===============================================================================

Alright, let's clean this signal.

The raw Emotiv data contains voltage spikes up to 715 thousand microvolts.
Real brain activity is in the range of plus or minus 50 microvolts. So
we are looking at noise that is four orders of magnitude larger than
the signal we want. We have to remove it.

Why do these spikes happen? Several reasons. An electrode might briefly
lose contact with the skin — that gives you a large DC step. Cable
movement creates triboelectric discharge. Muscle contractions produce
broadband bursts. And eye blinks themselves produce deflections on the
frontal channels around 200 microvolts — actually useful, but we need
to decide whether to keep them.

Our tool is the Interquartile Range method. We compute Q1 and Q3 for
each channel, take the IQR, and reject any sample where the distance
from the median exceeds three times the IQR.

Note: we use 3.0, not the textbook 1.5. Why? Because 1.5 would also
remove legitimate high-amplitude physiological events like alpha bursts
and K-complexes. 3.0 is conservative and targets only clear hardware
failures.

If any channel flags a row, we drop the entire row. This keeps the 14
channels aligned in time.

The result is dramatic. Before: skewness up to 78, kurtosis up to 6,000.
After: skewness below 2, kurtosis below 12 on every channel. We removed
1,374 rows, which is 9.2% of the data. We kept 90.8%.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 8 — BUTTERWORTH BANDPASS FILTER: THEORY
===============================================================================

IQR removed the spikes. But we still have two problems: slow DC drift
below 0.5 Hz, and high-frequency noise above 45 Hz. For that, we use a
Butterworth bandpass filter.

A Butterworth filter is characterised by having a maximally flat passband.
That means within the frequencies we want to keep, the filter does not
distort the amplitude. We used fourth-order, passband from 0.5 to 45 Hz,
applied with filtfilt for zero phase distortion.

Why Butterworth and not something else? There were three candidates.
Chebyshev has a sharper roll-off, but it ripples in the passband — which
would distort our band-power features. Elliptic is even sharper but
ripples in both bands — worse still. Butterworth is perfectly flat. For
EEG, amplitude fidelity matters more than having a razor-sharp cutoff.

What gets removed? Below 0.5 Hz, we eliminate DC drift caused by skin
impedance changes, sweat, and head movement. That is not neural activity.
Above 45 Hz, we remove 50 Hz European power-line interference, EMG
muscle contamination, and digital aliasing.

What is preserved? All five EEG bands: Delta at 0.5 to 4 Hz, Theta at
4 to 8, Alpha at 8 to 12, Beta at 12 to 30, and low Gamma at 30 to 45.

Technically, full gamma extends to 100 Hz, but Nyquist at 64 Hz and
line noise at 50 Hz constrain us to low gamma.

SLIDE 9 — BUTTERWORTH BANDPASS: EFFECT ON THE SIGNAL
===============================================================================

Let me show you this in action on channel O1, the occipital electrode.

Look at the top panel. This is the signal AFTER IQR spike removal but
BEFORE the bandpass filter. You can see it ranges from about 1,060 to
1,135 microvolts. The mean is around 1,098 microvolts. That is the DC
offset. It looks noisy, but the real neural oscillations are buried

Now look at the bottom panel. Same channel, same time window, but now
AFTER the bandpass filter. The range has collapsed to plus or minus

oscillatory structure of brain activity.

This is the signal our classifiers will actually see.

Three analysis cards below summarise: the top panel is spike-free but
drift-dominated. The bottom panel reveals the neural oscillation. The
filter introduces zero phase distortion because we used filtfilt, and
attenuation at the stopband edges is around 36 dB at 0.25 Hz and 24 dB
at 55 Hz — clean passband for physiological bands.

SLIDE 10 — WHY LOG-NORMALIZATION WAS REJECTED
===============================================================================

Here is a small methodological story worth telling.

We tested a third preprocessing step. Log-normalization. Many signal
processing pipelines apply a log transform to compress dynamic range
and reduce skewness. It's a common default.

The formula is x_new equals sign of x times the logarithm of one plus
the absolute value of x. It is supposed to symmetrise heavy-tailed
distributions.

So we measured it. On all 14 channels. And here is what we found: the
log transform WORSENED skewness on every single channel.

Take channel AF3 as an example. Before the log, skewness was minus 0.12,
which is nearly Gaussian. After the log, it became minus 0.94 — much
worse.

Why? Because our post-bandpass EEG is zero-mean and bipolar. The log
function expands values near zero and compresses large magnitudes.
That's the opposite of what a Gaussian-like signal needs. Log transform
artificially splits the distribution into two clumps around plus and
minus log of one.

Decision: we rejected log-normalization.

The lesson is: never apply a transform reflexively. Always measure.
We almost made things worse.

SLIDE 11 — FEATURE ENGINEERING: HEMISPHERIC ASYMMETRY
===============================================================================

Now let's talk about the features we engineered on top of the raw signal.

The first family is hemispheric asymmetry. The brain is functionally
lateralised. Research going back to Davidson in 1992 shows that the
difference between left and right frontal alpha correlates with emotional
valence. And reduced hemispheric symmetry is a biomarker for fatigue
and cognitive load.

For each of 7 symmetric electrode pairs, we computed left minus right.
Then for each pair, we ran a two-sample t-test comparing the open and
closed distributions.

The results are in the table on the right. Only two pairs are
statistically significant at alpha 0.05:

F7 minus F8 — p-value 0.018. The mean flips sign: positive 1.87 when
eyes are open, negative 0.94 when closed. Strong lateral frontal
asymmetry linked to eye state.

And P7 minus P8 — p-value 0.034. The sign also flips. This reflects
parietal attention redistribution when visual input ceases.

The other five pairs are not individually discriminative. Despite this,
we kept all 7 asymmetry features in our feature set. Weakly significant
features may still contribute to multivariate models through
interactions. Univariate p-values are too conservative a filter.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 12 — FEATURE ENGINEERING: BAND POWER AND BERGER EFFECT
===============================================================================

The second family of features is frequency band power.

For each channel, we computed the average power in each of five bands,
using Welch's method. The key question: how does band power differ
between eye-open and eye-closed?

Here is the closed-over-open ratio for each band:

Delta at 1.26 — the biggest ratio.
Theta at 1.03 — basically unchanged.
Alpha at 1.13 — this is the Berger effect signature.
Beta at 1.06 — slight increase.
Gamma at 0.98 — basically unchanged.

So let me tell you about Berger. Hans Berger invented EEG in 1924. He
published his discovery of the alpha rhythm in 1929. He noticed that
alpha waves at 8 to 12 Hz are suppressed when you open your eyes, and
they rebound dramatically when you close your eyes. This happens
because the thalamocortical loop between the LGN and the primary visual
cortex synchronises when it has no visual input to process.

In published research-grade EEG, the alpha closed-over-open ratio is
typically 2 to 5 times. Our ratio is 1.13.

Why is our signal so weak? Three reasons. Consumer-grade saline
electrodes have higher impedance. The subject was not instructed to
sustain closure and may have just been blinking. And the referential
montage used by Emotiv reduces signal-to-noise ratio compared to the
bipolar montage used in research.

The impact on our models: the "easy" physiological signal is muted. Our
classifiers cannot rely on a single strong alpha spike. They have to
exploit subtle multi-channel interactions.

SLIDE 16 — EDA: VIOLIN PLOTS

Violin plots combine a box plot with a kernel density estimate. Where

Why does this matter? Because it answers a question box plots cannot:

In our data, every channel is unimodal and approximately symmetric.

Subtle differences between open and closed: the closed violins on O1

SLIDE 17 — CORRELATION HEATMAP: RAW SIGNAL (PROBLEMATIC)
===============================================================================

This slide should make you concerned.

What you are looking at is the correlation matrix computed on raw
signal, winsorised at the first and ninety-ninth percentile just to
make the colours readable. Even after clipping, many cells are
saturated red — correlations above 0.8.

Why so many high correlations? Three reasons.

First, shared spikes. When one electrode pops, multiple channels
register the spike simultaneously. That creates artificial covariance.

Second, the common mode reference. All 14 channels share the same
reference electrode — CMS at location P3. When a large drift hits the
reference, every channel records the subtracted version, and that
creates correlation across all derivations.

Third, DC offset. Channel means co-vary slowly due to environmental
factors like skin impedance and temperature, and this inflates Pearson
correlation on raw data.

The danger is this: if we trained a classifier on this raw matrix, it
would learn to exploit artifacts rather than physiology. It would
perform beautifully on this data and fail on any other recording.

Raw data lies. Preprocessing reveals truth.

SLIDE 19 — COLLINEARITY: WHY PAIRS STAY COUPLED
===============================================================================

A natural question after the previous slide: F7 and F8 still correlate
at 0.87. Is that a problem?

The answer is no, and here is why.

That correlation is physiological, not artifactual. There's a
neuroanatomical reason and a functional reason.

Anatomically, the corpus callosum has over 200 million axons directly
connecting the two hemispheres. Frontal cortex activity is tightly
coupled bilaterally during routine cognition. Functionally, both
frontal lobes participate in the same executive networks — attention,
inhibition, working memory — and they receive common top-down input
from the anterior cingulate and basal ganglia.

The correlation is real information, not redundancy. If we removed one
of the pair, we would lose the ability to compute the asymmetry
features that turned out to be discriminative.

Model-specific implications. Some models are sensitive to collinearity:
Logistic Regression becomes unstable when correlations exceed 0.9,
LDA's covariance matrix becomes near-singular, linear SVMs diverge.
Other models are robust: Random Forest randomly samples square-root-of-p
features per split and implicitly breaks collinearity. Gradient
boosting explores alternative features. Neural networks absorb
redundancy via distributed representations.

Our solution: use L2 regularisation on LogReg and SVM. Tree models
handle it natively. No feature is dropped.

SLIDE 20 — SPECTRAL ANALYSIS: FFT THEORY
===============================================================================

Now we transition from the time domain to the frequency domain. This
is crucial for EEG because brain activity is fundamentally oscillatory.

The Fast Fourier Transform is the tool. The core idea is that any
finite signal can be expressed as a sum of sinusoids. FFT computes
the amplitude and phase of each one.

The formula is X of k equals the sum over n of x of n times the complex
exponential of minus j 2 pi k n over N. Don't worry about the formula
too much. What matters is: FFT gives you the strength of every
frequency component in your signal.

The algorithm used in practice is Cooley-Tukey, published in 1965. It
reduces computational complexity from order N-squared to order N log N.
That is why real-time spectral analysis became possible.

For our data: sampling rate is 128 Hz, so the Nyquist frequency is 64
Hz. That means we can accurately measure frequencies up to 64 Hz and
no higher. We use a Hann window to reduce spectral leakage.

Why is frequency analysis right for EEG? Because different brain states
are characterised by different frequency bands, not different
time-domain amplitudes. A drowsy brain and an alert brain look similar
in time, but radically different in frequency.

This weakness is a major reason our classification is harder than on

===============================================================================
SLIDE 22 — POWER SPECTRAL DENSITY: WELCH'S METHOD
===============================================================================

This is our most informative spectral plot. It uses Welch's method,
which is a refinement of basic FFT.

The procedure: split the signal into overlapping segments — 50%
overlap, with a Hann window. Compute the FFT of each segment, square
the magnitude to get power. Then average across segments. This
averaging reduces the variance of the spectral estimate.

The result is a much smoother, more reliable PSD. A single FFT is
noisy; many averaged are smooth.

What the chart shows: the closed-eye PSD shown in red sits clearly
above the open-eye PSD shown in blue. This is visible across the
Delta, Theta, Alpha, and Beta bands. It is most prominent on
occipital and parietal channels. And the separation is largest in the
4 to 15 Hz range.

This confirms, visually, what we measured numerically on slide 12:
higher power when eyes are closed, with the biggest effect in the
lower frequencies.

===============================================================================

Four takeaways that will guide every subsequent modelling decision.

Takeaway one: the 1/f background is confirmed across all 14 channels.

Takeaway two: the alpha bump is weak. A ratio of 1.13 when literature

SLIDE 24 — DIMENSIONALITY REDUCTION OVERVIEW
===============================================================================

We now have a 116-dimensional feature vector per sample: 14 raw channels,
14 rolling means, 14 rolling standard deviations, 70 FFT features, and
4 band ratios.

To visualise the class structure, we need to project 116 dimensions
down to 1, 2, or 3. We use three complementary methods.

Linear Discriminant Analysis — LDA — is supervised and linear. It finds
the single direction that maximises the ratio of between-class scatter
to within-class scatter. For binary classification, it produces exactly
one discriminant axis.

t-SNE is unsupervised and non-linear. It preserves only local
neighbourhood structure. Great for revealing clusters.

UMAP is also unsupervised and non-linear, but it preserves both local
and global structure. It's more scalable and more deterministic than
t-SNE.

We measured clustering quality with three metrics: Silhouette, where
higher is better; Davies-Bouldin, where lower is better; and
Calinski-Harabasz, where higher is better.

Verdict: LDA wins on all three metrics. This makes sense — only LDA
uses the class labels during projection. Supervised dominates
unsupervised when classes exist.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 25 — LDA DEEP DIVE
===============================================================================

Let me explain LDA in a bit more detail because it's the winner.

Fisher's criterion — the mathematical objective of LDA — asks: can I
find a direction w in the high-dimensional space such that when I
project the data onto w, the two classes are maximally separated?

Formally, the objective function J(w) is the ratio of w-transpose S_B
w, the between-class scatter, over w-transpose S_W w, the within-class
scatter. Maximise this, and you get the most discriminative axis.

There is a closed-form solution: w is an eigenvector of S_W-inverse
S_B. For binary classification, you get exactly one direction.

Looking at our chart: the x-axis is the LD1 projection value. The
y-axis is sample density, estimated by a kernel density smoother. Blue
is eye-open, red is eye-closed. The two peaks are displaced by about
1.5 standard deviations. Modest but real separation.

The Silhouette score of 0.1556 tells us: positive silhouette means
points are, on average, closer to their own class centroid than to
the other. A linear boundary IS separating the classes, just with
significant overlap.

And that is why Logistic Regression — a linear model — is our best
machine learning baseline. The LDA result predicted it.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 26 — T-SNE DEEP DIVE
===============================================================================

t-SNE stands for t-distributed Stochastic Neighbour Embedding. It's
the most popular non-linear dimensionality reduction method.

The core idea: preserve pairwise similarities between nearby points,
and throw away information about far-apart points.

The algorithm has two steps. In the high-dimensional space, compute
pairwise similarity probabilities using a Gaussian kernel — the
perplexity parameter controls the kernel width, typically 30. In the
low-dimensional space, place points to minimise the KL divergence
between the high-dimensional and low-dimensional similarities. The
low-dim similarity uses a heavy-tailed t-distribution, which prevents
the so-called crowding problem.

Our parameters: perplexity 30, learning rate auto, initialisation by
PCA for determinism.

What does the plot show? Red and blue points overlap heavily. There
are small local sub-clusters, but they do not correspond to class
labels. This tells us that class information is NOT carried by local
feature-space neighbourhoods.

Important caveats for any t-SNE plot. Results vary with random seed
and perplexity. Never interpret cluster sizes or inter-cluster
distances as meaningful. t-SNE is a visualisation tool, not an
analysis tool.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 27 — UMAP DEEP DIVE
===============================================================================

UMAP — Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection — is the modern
alternative to t-SNE, published in 2018.

The theoretical foundation is quite deep. UMAP assumes that data lies
on a Riemannian manifold. It constructs a fuzzy simplicial complex in
high dimensions, then finds the low-dimensional embedding that best
preserves that topology.

The algorithm has three steps. Build a k-nearest-neighbour graph in
high dimensions, default k equals 15. Assign fuzzy edge weights based
on local connectivity. Optimise the low-dimensional layout via
cross-entropy minimisation.

Three advantages over t-SNE. First, UMAP preserves global structure,
so inter-cluster distances are meaningful. Second, it is deterministic
given a seed — the result is reproducible. Third, it scales to
millions of points, whereas t-SNE is order n-squared.

What we see in the plot: slightly better visual separation than t-SNE,
Silhouette 0.065 versus 0.055, with a thin gradient between classes.
This is consistent with eye state being a continuous variable rather
than a sharp dichotomy. Makes physiological sense too — eye closing
and opening is a gradual process in real life.

Conclusion across all three dimensionality reduction methods: class
structure in our data is weak. Supervision is essential.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 28 — MACHINE LEARNING: METHODOLOGY
===============================================================================

We are now entering the machine learning section. Five classical
algorithms: Logistic Regression, SVM with RBF kernel, Random Forest,
Gradient Boosting, and XGBoost.

Our experimental protocol. We used three train-validate-test splits,
all chronological, no shuffling. 60/20/20, 70/15/15, 80/10/10. Running
all three lets us check stability across splits.

Why chronological? Because of the temporal concept drift we established
on slide 6. The last 15% of the recording has a radically different
class distribution. Random shuffling would destroy this signal and
give misleadingly optimistic results.

Class imbalance was handled two ways. First, balanced class weights.
Second, decision thresholds were optimised on the validation set by
grid search from 0.1 to 0.9 in steps of 0.05.

Our primary metric is Macro-F1 — the average of the F1 scores for the
open and closed classes. Unlike accuracy, Macro-F1 weighs both classes
equally. That is robust to the imbalanced test partition.

Secondary metrics: accuracy, which is inflated by imbalance and
treated with caution; Binary-F1 on the closed class, which tracks
minority-class quality; and AUC-ROC, which is threshold-independent.

One important caveat about AUC. Because test priors differ from train,
AUC can invert — drop below 0.5 — when models rank correctly on CV
but reverse on test. This is a hallmark of concept drift, and we will
see it happen.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 29 — LOGISTIC REGRESSION
===============================================================================

Algorithm one of five: Logistic Regression. And as you'll see, this
ended up being the best of the classical machine learning models.

The model is simple. P of y equals 1, given x, equals the sigmoid of
w-transpose x plus b. The sigmoid is 1 over 1 plus e to the minus z.
The loss function is binary cross-entropy: minus the sum over samples
of y log p plus one minus y log one minus p.

This loss function is convex — meaning there is a unique global
minimum. We use the L-BFGS solver, which is efficient.

Hyperparameters we used. L2 penalty, also known as Ridge regularisation.
C equals 1, the inverse regularisation strength. Balanced class
weights. Maximum iterations 1000.

Results in the table. On the 60/20/20 split, Macro-F1 was 0.478. On
70/15/15, 0.454. On 80/10/10, 0.464. Mean across splits: 0.4665. That
is the best mean Macro-F1 of any classical ML model.

Why did it win? Three reasons. First, lowest variance across splits —
standard deviation of only 0.013. That means Logistic Regression is
the most STABLE model under temporal drift. Second, L2 regularisation
damps the coefficients of collinear frontal features. Third, balanced
class weights force the decision boundary away from the majority class.

Why is AUC so low? Because the train-test class reshuffling inverts
the learned ranking. Macro-F1 survives threshold tuning. AUC does not.

Best for: production systems requiring interpretability, fast
inference, calibrated probabilities, and stability under distribution
shift.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 30 — SVM WITH RBF KERNEL
===============================================================================

Algorithm two of five: Support Vector Machine with a Gaussian RBF kernel.
Competitive but less stable than Logistic Regression.

The SVM is a maximum-margin classifier. In its dual form, the decision
function is the sum over training points of alpha-i times y-i times
K of x-i and x, plus a bias term. The magic is in K, the kernel.

The RBF kernel is the Gaussian kernel: K of x-i and x-j equals the
exponential of minus gamma times the squared distance between them.
This implicitly maps the data to an infinite-dimensional feature
space. In that space, the SVM can fit any continuous decision boundary.

Optimisation is done by quadratic programming. Convex, but memory scales
as n-squared to n-cubed, making SVM impractical for more than about
100,000 samples.

Our hyperparameters. Kernel RBF. C equals 1. Gamma set to "scale",
which is 1 divided by n-features times the variance of X. Probability
equals true so we can compute AUC via Platt scaling. Class weight
balanced.

Results. Mean Macro-F1 across splits: 0.4233. That's lower than
Logistic Regression's 0.4665.

Why did SVM underperform? The RBF kernel essentially MEMORISES the
local curvature of the training distribution. When concept drift shifts
the test distribution, the learned curvature no longer applies. High
variance across splits — Macro-F1 ranged from 0.389 to 0.480.

When does SVM shine? On the 80/10/10 split it hit 0.92 accuracy.
When train and test are temporally close, the non-linear boundary
captures real structure.

Best for: smaller datasets with stationary distributions, where
non-linear separation is needed and top accuracy matters more than
stability.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 31 — RANDOM FOREST
===============================================================================

Algorithm three of five: Random Forest. An ensemble of 200 decorrelated
decision trees.

The prediction rule is majority vote across the 200 trees. Each tree
is trained on a bootstrap sample of rows — sampled with replacement.
At each node, the tree considers only the square-root of the total
feature count — not all 14 channels. And it splits using Gini impurity
minimisation. Gini equals 1 minus the sum over classes of p-class
squared.

The key property is that bagging plus random feature selection
DECORRELATES the trees. Bagging reduces variance; feature randomness
breaks collinearity. This is why RF handles correlated features
natively — something Logistic Regression cannot do without
regularisation.

Hyperparameters. 200 estimators. Max depth none, so trees grow to
purity. Minimum 2 samples per split. Balanced class weights.

Random Forest also provides feature importance. And as we'll see on
slide 33, F7, F8, FC5, AF3, and AF4 dominate — all lateral frontal
channels. These pick up eye-blink EMG artifacts, which correlate with
eye state. The model has found that blinks are a strong proxy.

Results. Mean Macro-F1 across splits: 0.4311. Rank 6 overall.

Why competitive but not winning? Pluses: handles collinearity,
no scaling needed, built-in variance reduction. Minuses: trees grown
to purity overfit the training distribution. Bagging reduces variance
but not bias against distribution shift.

Best for: tabular data with heterogeneous features, when interpretability
via feature importance is required, and when training time is not
critical.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 32 — GRADIENT BOOSTING AND XGBOOST
===============================================================================

Algorithms four and five: Gradient Boosting and XGBoost. Two boosting
ensembles on one slide, because they share the core principle.

The shared principle is sequential additive model construction. At
each step, F-m of x equals F-m-minus-1 of x plus eta times h-m of x,
where h-m is a new tree that fits the NEGATIVE GRADIENT of the loss
evaluated at the previous ensemble.

Each new tree corrects the previous ensemble's residual errors. The
learning rate eta — shrinkage — controls how much each tree contributes.

Contrast with Random Forest: RF trees are INDEPENDENT and grown in
parallel. Gradient boosting trees are SEQUENTIAL and cumulatively
correct errors.

On the left: scikit-learn's Gradient Boosting. Configuration: 200
trees, learning rate 0.1, max depth 5. Mean Macro-F1 across splits:
0.4723. Rank 4 overall. The key observation: LOWEST variance of any
model — standard deviation of Macro-F1 is only 0.038. The depth-5
constraint prevents overfitting to spikes. Best for stable benchmarks.

On the right: XGBoost. Same configuration plus scale-pos-weight for
imbalance. Adds L1 and L2 regularisation on leaves, histogram-based
split finding for speed, and native missing-value handling. Mean
Macro-F1: 0.4363. Rank 8 overall.

Why is XGBoost slightly worse? Default regularisation is too
aggressive for our weak signal — it under-fits. Would benefit from
hyperparameter search. XGBoost is not automatically better than
scikit-learn GBM. For small, noisy datasets, simpler regularisation
often wins.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 33 — ML COMPARISON: ROC AND FEATURE IMPORTANCE
===============================================================================

This slide visualises all five ML results together.

On the left, the ROC curves for the 70/15/15 test. And here is the
striking observation: all five curves sit BELOW the diagonal. AUC
values are in the 0.36 to 0.40 range. The models rank WORSE than
random on this test.

Important: this does not mean the models are bad. It means the test
distribution has inverted relative to the training distribution. The
last quartile of our recording has only 8% closed-eye samples, while
the training data had roughly 50%. Models learned one signal direction
during training; the test data requires the opposite. AUC inverts.

On the right, the RandomForest feature importance. The top five
features are F7 at 0.084, F8 at 0.078, FC5 at 0.077, AF3 at 0.076,
and AF4 at 0.073. All lateral frontal channels. These are where
eye-blink electromyography is strongest. Eye blinks correlate with
eye state, so the model learned to classify via blinks — a strong
proxy signal.

And here is the most surprising finding of the report: the occipital
channels O1 and O2, despite being the classical Berger effect sites,
rank only mid-table — 9th and 13th. The physiological alpha signal is
so weak in our data that blink artifacts are easier for the model to
learn.

Models find the shortest path to the objective. If blinks are easier
than alpha, models will use blinks.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 34 — READING TRAIN, CV, AND TEST LOSS CURVES
===============================================================================

Before I show you the deep learning results, I want to take a moment
to teach you how to read loss curves. This slide is a diagnostic
guide.

There are six patterns you will see in practice, and recognising each
is essential.

Pattern one: healthy fit. Train and CV curves decrease together and
plateau near each other. Small gap. Generalisation is good. Stop
training briefly after the plateau.

Pattern two: overfitting. Train loss keeps decreasing, but CV loss
starts rising after some epoch. The gap widens. This is what our
LSTM and CNN-LSTM exhibit. The remedy: early stopping, dropout, more
data, or smaller model capacity.

Pattern three: underfitting. Both curves plateau at high values,
nearly identical and flat. This is what our Transformer shows. Either
the model is too small or the feature signal is too weak. The remedy:
bigger model, better features, tune learning rate.

Pattern four: learning rate too high. Losses oscillate violently or
diverge. Train loss may spike randomly. The optimiser is bouncing out
of loss basins. Reduce learning rate by 10 times, use cosine
annealing or warmup.

Pattern five: class collapse. Train loss drops briefly then flatlines.
CV loss flat at a different level. All predictions become the
majority class. A trivial solution. Remedies: balanced loss,
threshold tuning, re-sampling.

Pattern six: pathological noisy CV. CV loss oscillates wildly while
train descends smoothly. This is what our PatchTST shows. Usually
means the CV set is too small or the model has too many parameters.
Remedies: more CV data, fewer parameters, ensembling.

Keep this slide in mind as we look at each of our deep learning
models.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 35 — DEEP LEARNING ARCHITECTURES AND PROTOCOL
===============================================================================

Our five deep learning architectures. From heaviest to lightest:

LSTM at 200,000 parameters — recurrent.
CNN-LSTM at 150,000 — hybrid.
EEG Transformer at 80,000 — attention-based.
PatchTST Lite at 50,000 — patch-based transformer.
And EEGNet at only 400 parameters — domain-specific CNN.

Yes, you heard that right. EEGNet has 400 parameters. 500 times fewer
than LSTM. And it ended up winning.

All five models share the same training configuration. Loss is
weighted binary cross-entropy, with class weights set to N divided by
2 N-c. Optimiser is AdamW, learning rate 1e-3, weight decay 1e-4.
Learning rate scheduler is cosine annealing over 25 epochs, smoothly
decaying to zero at the end.

The input window is 64 samples — about 500 milliseconds at 128 Hz.
That is long enough to capture two full alpha cycles.

Batch size 64, 25 epochs, no early stopping. We deliberately kept
training going to the end so the loss curves would be instructive.

Post-training, we sweep decision thresholds from 0.1 to 0.95 on the
validation set and pick the arg-max of Macro-F1.

Identical configuration across all five architectures is deliberate.
It is a controlled experiment: any performance difference is caused
by the architecture alone.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 36 — LSTM
===============================================================================

Deep learning algorithm one of five: LSTM, Long Short-Term Memory.

The architecture is two stacked bidirectional LSTMs with 64 hidden
units each, followed by average pooling, dropout of 0.3, and a dense
output layer.

The LSTM cell is defined by four equations. The forget gate decides
what to keep or drop from memory. The input gate decides what new
information to add. The cell state carries information forward. And
the output gate decides what to output.

The whole point of LSTM is addressing vanishing gradients — by
controlling information flow via gates, gradients can propagate
through long sequences.

Why bidirectional? The backward pass lets the model use FUTURE context.
For eye-state boundary detection, transitions extend before and after
the moment, so future context is valuable.

Total parameters: 200,000. Our heaviest model.

Results on the 70/15/15 split. Macro-F1: 0.4177. AUC: 0.5908. Accuracy:
0.5655. Threshold: 0.92.

Why these results? Look at the loss curve. Train loss drops to 0.04 by
epoch 25 — nearly zero. CV loss rises from 0.84 to 2.86. That is
severe overfitting. The model memorised the training set. Its 200,000
parameters had nothing else to do with only 9,500 training samples.

Best for: long time-series where temporal order is the main information
and where data volume is sufficient — over 100,000 samples — to
prevent overfitting.

Caveats here: our dataset is too small for this capacity.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 37 — CNN-LSTM
===============================================================================

Deep learning algorithm two of five: CNN-LSTM. A hybrid architecture.

The idea: put 1D convolutional layers in front of the LSTM. The CNN
extracts local temporal features, and the LSTM then models the
sequence of those features.

Architecture: Input of 64 by 14 goes through a Conv1D with 32 filters
of kernel size 5, ReLU, max pool. Then another Conv1D with 64 filters
of kernel size 3, ReLU, max pool. Then a bidirectional LSTM with 64
hidden units. Dropout 0.3. Dense output.

The Conv1D is effectively a learned filter bank. Each filter learns a
band-pass-like pattern. Kernel size 5 covers about 40 milliseconds,
which is long enough to capture a single oscillatory burst.

Why combine CNN with LSTM? Because local patterns and long-range
context both matter for EEG. The CNN extracts features; the LSTM
models how features are arranged in time. This hybrid is standard in
speech recognition and often used in biosignal analysis.

Total parameters: 150,000.

Results. Macro-F1: 0.4837, a big jump from LSTM's 0.4177. AUC: 0.7119,
up from 0.59. Accuracy: 0.7086. Threshold: 0.82.

The CNN front-end learned noise-robust features before temporal
integration, and that improved AUC substantially. It still overfits —
CV loss reaches 2.3 — but much less severely than plain LSTM.

Best for: time series where local pattern recognition and long-range
context both matter — speech, ECG, EEG, IMU sensors.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 38 — EEG TRANSFORMER
===============================================================================

Deep learning algorithm three of five: the EEG Transformer. Our most
visible failure, and it's instructive.

The architecture uses a CLS token with sinusoidal positional encoding
and three Transformer encoder layers. Each encoder has multi-head
self-attention with 4 heads, feature dimension 32, feedforward
dimension 128, dropout 0.1.

The self-attention formula: Attn of Q, K, V equals softmax of QK
transpose divided by square-root of d_k, times V. The softmax creates
a weighted combination of value vectors, where the weights come from
how similar query and key are.

Why attention for EEG? In theory, every time-step attends to every
other time-step, capturing long-range dependencies. And attention can
also operate across electrodes, picking up spatial interactions
without hand-crafted features.

Parameters: 80,000. The CLS token is a learnable summary vector,
BERT-style, that aggregates the entire sequence into one classification
vector.

Results. Macro-F1: 0.3806. AUC: 0.1221 — WORSE than random. Accuracy:
0.6146.

What happened? The loss curve shows classic class collapse. CV
Macro-F1 was FROZEN at 0.2702 across all 25 epochs. The model learned
to predict the majority class only. Attention is powerful, but it
needs massive data to shine.

With about 9,500 training samples and 80,000 parameters, there was no
inductive bias to exploit the weak label signal. Remedies would
include pre-training on unlabelled EEG, adding a convolution front-end,
or stronger regularisation.

Best for: large datasets — millions of samples — or tasks where
attention maps are the scientific goal.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 39 — EEGNET: THE WINNER
===============================================================================

Deep learning algorithm four of five: EEGNet. Published by Lawhern and
colleagues in 2018. And this is the overall winner.

Let me repeat: it has only 400 parameters. And it won the mean
Macro-F1 comparison at 0.4964.

The architecture has three blocks. Block 1 is a 2D convolution with
temporal kernels — 1 by 64, 8 filters — followed by batch
normalisation. Block 2 is a depthwise 2D convolution with spatial
kernels — 14 by 1, depth multiplier 2 — batch norm, ELU, average
pool, dropout. Block 3 is a separable convolution of 1 by 16, batch
norm, ELU, pool, dropout, dense output.

Two key innovations. Depthwise convolution applies ONE filter per
channel instead of mixing channels. This matches EEG physics — each
electrode captures a different brain region, so we should not blend
them. Separable convolution combines depthwise plus a 1 by 1 pointwise
layer, reducing parameters by 8 to 10 times while preserving
expressiveness.

Results. Macro-F1 on the test set: 0.4977. Mean Macro-F1 across splits:
0.4964. Our winner. AUC: 0.7597, the highest of any model we tested.
Accuracy: 0.6859. Threshold: 0.65, close to the ideal midpoint of 0.5.

Why did EEGNet win despite being tiny? Only 400 parameters, so it
literally CANNOT overfit 9,500 training samples. The temporal filter
acts like a learned band-pass per channel. The depthwise spatial
filter learns optimal electrode weightings. And the threshold stays
naturally close to 0.5, which means the decision boundary generalises.

Best for: any EEG classification task — drowsiness, BCI, motor
imagery, sleep staging. This is the canonical choice for EEG datasets
with fewer than 100,000 samples.

Inductive bias beats brute force. 400 parameters with the right
structure outperform 200,000 without.


===============================================================================
SLIDE 40 — PATCHTST LITE
===============================================================================

Deep learning algorithm five of five: PatchTST Lite. Published by Nie
and colleagues in 2023, originally designed for long-horizon time
series forecasting.

The architecture. The input of 64 by 14 is split into 15 overlapping
patches of size 8 with stride 4 — each patch is about 62 milliseconds.
Each patch is linearly embedded into a 32-dimensional token. Add
positional encoding, prepend a CLS token, run through 2 Transformer
encoder layers, extract the CLS, dense output.

The key innovation of PatchTST: rather than having a token for each
time step — which would scale quadratically with sequence length —
have a token for each patch. Attention complexity drops from order
64-squared to order 15-squared. This enables long-context modelling
at reasonable cost.

Parameters: 50,000 for our lightweight variant.

Results. Macro-F1: 0.2820. AUC: 0.2221. Accuracy: 0.3728. Threshold:
0.95, an extreme value.

These numbers are the worst of any DL model. But — and this is
important — PatchTST has a unique property that makes it valuable for
one specific use case. It produces almost zero FALSE NEGATIVES. The
model predicts "closed" aggressively. It misses very few closed-eye
events. The trade-off is many false positives.

Best for: safety-critical BCI where missing a closed-eye event is
catastrophic — drowsy driver detection is the canonical example. Also
long time-series forecasting, its original purpose.

Low overall metrics don't mean useless. PatchTST optimises for FN
equals zero, which is exactly what a drowsy-driver alert system needs.

SLIDE 42 — WHICH ALGORITHM IS BEST: DECISION MATRIX
===============================================================================

Performance is not one-dimensional. Different scenarios favour
different models. Let me walk you through our decision matrix.

For best overall accuracy and F1: EEGNet. Mean Macro-F1 of 0.4964 and
highest AUC of 0.76.

For stable, predictable production: Logistic Regression. Lowest
standard deviation across splits. Fastest inference. Calibrated

For safety-critical applications where you need to minimise false

If you need feature importance for interpretability: Random Forest.
Native feature ranking, which identifies F7, F8, and AF3 as the key

For a low-variance benchmark baseline: Gradient Boosting. Lowest
variance ML model, robust to hyperparameter choices.

For limited compute or embedded deployment: EEGNet again. Only 400
parameters. Runs on a microcontroller. Best accuracy-per-parameter.

For local non-linear patterns with stationary data: SVM-RBF. When
train and test are temporally close, it hits 0.92 accuracy.

For maximum capacity with very large datasets: LSTM or Transformer.
With over 100,000 samples, recurrent and attention models scale better
than EEGNet.

There is no single "best" model. The best depends on the question and
the constraints. Always ask: best at what?

SLIDE 43 — KEY PROBLEMS IDENTIFIED
===============================================================================

Honest limitations that bound our performance. Nine problems, in order.

Temporal concept drift. The last 15% of the recording is only 8.1%
closed. The 44.9% distribution shift makes accuracy misleading.

Severe overfitting on LSTM and CNN-LSTM. Train loss drops to 0.04;
CV loss rises to 2.86. CV Macro-F1 plateaus around 0.60 but test
collapses to 0.42.

Transformer stuck at baseline. CV Macro-F1 frozen at 0.2702 across
25 epochs. Predicts majority class only. AUC 0.12.

Near-chance test AUC. All five ML models have AUC between 0.20 and
0.40 on the 70/15/15 test. Rankings invert between CV and test.

Unstable CV-optimised thresholds. The optimal threshold varies from
0.53 to 0.95 for the same model across splits. No single threshold
transfers reliably.

Walk-forward fold degeneracy. Fold 1 validation is 100% closed. Fold
3 is only 0.97% closed. AUC is undefined on homogeneous folds.

Single-subject dataset. N equals 14,980 from ONE session with ONE
subject. No cross-subject validation. Results do not generalise.

Weak alpha-band separation. Alpha ratio 1.13 versus literature 2 to
5 times. Physiological signal is below the floor most papers assume.

Low Binary-F1 on the closed class. Best DL Binary-F1 is 0.21 on
EEGNet. Most models below 0.15. Closed-eye events are rarely
predicted correctly at test.

Strong presentations include their limitations. This is not weakness
— it is scientific honesty and points the way for future work.
